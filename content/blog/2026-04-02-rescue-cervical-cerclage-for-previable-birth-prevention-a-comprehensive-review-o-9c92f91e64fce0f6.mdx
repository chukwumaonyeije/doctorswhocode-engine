It’s 18:40 on a Tuesday. Triage rolls in a 20+5 patient with membranes at the os, 2–3 cm dilated. Her ultrasound at 10:15 reported a 7 mm cervix. The note hit the chart at 15:42 as a scanned PDF. Authorization for progesterone was “pending.”

We called it bad luck. It wasn’t. It was architecture.

The usual framing says rescue cerclage is a heroic, case-by-case save. That framing excuses delay, inconsistency, and preventable loss. Rescue isn’t a plan. A pathway is.

What keeps this broken is structural, not clinical skill. We have:
- Ultrasound reports locked in PDFs, not discrete data, so no automated trigger fires.
- Ambiguous “consider cerclage” language that protects the reader, not the patient.
- OR access governed by block time and politics, not time-to-cerclage.
- Anesthesia and NICU risk-aversion without a shared, versioned protocol.
- Reimbursement that pays for consults and documentation more than timely intervention.
- Weekend and after-hours entropy that turns minutes into miscarriages.

We can stop pretending variability is sophistication. It’s drift.

What the evidence says (and what it doesn’t)
- Abstract-only review: Cureus, 2024 (PMID: 39371694; PMCID: PMC11450839). It summarizes rescue cervical cerclage (RCC) indications, techniques (McDonald, Shirodkar), and outcomes in cervical insufficiency or marked shortening. It reports RCC reduces previable birth in selected patients, and lists risks like infection and cervical laceration.
- Completeness limits: It’s a narrative review, abstract-only here. No pooled effect sizes are presented in this record, and emergency RCC evidence is largely observational with selection bias. The call for better criteria is appropriate.

Even with partial evidence, the clinical reality is consistent: when the right patient gets timely cerclage with disciplined prep and follow-up, gestational gain improves. The failure mode is delay and indecision, not a lack of suture.

So build the pathway
This is work physicians must lead, because the domain edge cases drive the architecture. Here is a minimally viable, production-grade rapid cerclage pathway any hospital can stand up in 60–90 days.

1) Data model and triggers
- Capture cervical length and funneling as discrete fields at the point of ultrasound. No PDFs without structured data.
- Event-driven trigger when CL ≤10–15 mm before 24 weeks, or any painless dilation with visible membranes.
- One-click “Cerclage Pathway” activation from the result, triage, or clinic.

2) Eligibility and decision support (versioned, simple, enforced)
- Inclusion: singleton (or defined multi-criteria), 16–24 weeks, no chorioamnionitis signs, intact membranes, no major fetal anomaly incompatible with survival.
- Exclusion/defer: active labor, significant bleeding/PPROM, intrauterine infection, hemodynamic instability.
- Technique decision tree: McDonald default; Shirodkar for exposed cervix/low tissue purchase; abandon if infection suspected or anatomy unsafe. Show this in the order set, not a PDF guideline.

3) Single-call team activation
- Paged group: MFM/OB surgeon on call, anesthesia, L&D charge, OR charge, NICU, ultrasound, and blood bank.
- Target time-to-cerclage decision: ≤60 minutes from trigger.
- Target wheels-in-to-suture: ≤6 hours if eligible and stable.

4) Pre-op bundle (orderable as one)
- Broad-spectrum antibiotics per protocol, indomethacin if no contraindication, bladder emptying, Trendelenburg, sterile prep, tocolysis as needed.
- Standardized consent with absolute and relative risks (infection, laceration, PPROM, failure).
- Ultrasound confirmation and fetal status.

5) Intra-op discipline
- Checklist: suture type available, cerclage instrument set checked, ultrasound guidance available, second set for Shirodkar.
- Abort criteria defined and documented; no improvisational heroics that convert to harm.

6) Post-op and follow-up
- 24–48 hour observation protocol, infection surveillance, discharge plan with clear return precautions.
- Scheduled follow-up ultrasound cadence and progesterone plan if applicable.
- Removal criteria and plan documented at placement.

7) Observability and governance
- Metrics on a live dashboard: time-to-decision, time-to-OR, gestational age at placement, gestational gain, chorioamnionitis, PPROM within 7 days, cervical laceration, NICU days, survival to discharge.
- Risk adjustment: gestational age at presentation, dilation, membrane status.
- Monthly review with authority to change the protocol. Version every change. Publish it internally.

This isn’t theoretical. You can implement the triggers with your EHR’s rules engine or a small microservice listening for new ultrasound results via HL7/FHIR. Route a secure message to the activation group. Lock eligibility logic into the order set to prevent “soft” exclusions from becoming delay. Bind OR access with a policy: rapid cerclage cases preempt elective blocks within the 6-hour window. That governance is the point.

What changes when physicians build
- We convert “consider” into a timed decision with accountability.
- We stop debating the obvious at 2 a.m. because the protocol did the thinking at 2 p.m.
- We measure the thing that matters: gestational gain, not the elegance of the suture.
- We make after-hours care look like daytime care.

And we learn faster. The Cureus review calls for better criteria. Great—stand up a registry inside your pathway. Capture anatomy, dilation, membrane position, technique, operator, and outcomes. Share de-identified aggregates across centers. You don’t need a grant to count and improve.

Objections I no longer buy
- “Every case is unique.” Anatomy varies. The workflow should not.
- “We can’t automate judgment.” We’re not. We’re forcing timely judgment with the best available signal.
- “Block time is sacred.” Not more sacred than a viable neonate.
- “The evidence isn’t perfect.” It never is in emergencies. Build the system that generates it.

Conclusion
Rescue is not a plan. Architecture is. If we keep treating previable birth prevention as a heroic one-off, we’ll keep losing quiet, preventable weeks of gestation to paperwork, governance drift, and OR politics.

Physicians built the techniques. Now we have to build the pathway—data, triggers, bundles, metrics, and authority—or accept outcomes designed by inertia. The future of maternal-fetal care belongs to clinicians who ship systems, not just sutures.
