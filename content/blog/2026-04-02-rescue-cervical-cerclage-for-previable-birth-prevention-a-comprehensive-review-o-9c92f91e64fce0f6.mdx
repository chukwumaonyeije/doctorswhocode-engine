At 19 weeks and change, she rolled in with membranes peeking at the os and no records. Ultrasound, labs, consent, anesthesia, an OR that didn’t exist five minutes ago. We threaded a stitch and bought weeks. We shouldn’t be winging this at 2 a.m. with luck and phone trees.

The usual debate—McDonald vs Shirodkar, tocolysis or not, progesterone after or before—is a distraction. The real variable is time-to-cerclage and system reliability. Rescue cervical cerclage (RCC) works best when we identify the right patient and move without friction. That’s an operations problem pretending to be a surgical one.

What the evidence actually says
- A 2024 Cureus review (abstract-only; PMID: 39371694) reports that RCC reduces previable birth in selected patients with cervical dilation or marked shortening, with risks like infection and cervical laceration. Techniques vary; success varies. Compared with progesterone or “bed rest,” RCC can extend pregnancy when the cervix is failing.
- That’s the useful core. The rest is heterogeneity and inconsistent criteria. We don’t have a universal algorithm. We have enough signal to act, and we know the harms of delay.

Why the problem persists
- We don’t run cervical length like a monitored vital sign. No default surveillance, no live alerts, no shared thresholds. So the “rescue” happens late.
- OR access and anesthesia sit outside OB governance. One “add-on” can become a three-hour wait. Infection risk climbs while we negotiate block time.
- EHRs lack a single-click pathway. No order set, no consent template, no timeout checklist, no device inventory tracking. Each case starts from zero.
- Training is variable. Some residents see one prolapsed-membrane case before graduation; others see none. Technique debates substitute for system design.
- Quality metrics punish preterm birth rates but rarely measure door-to-stitch time, latency gained, or equitable access. Incentives reward documentation, not execution.

What changes when physicians build
We don’t need another position paper. We need a working pathway, encoded in the EHR and guaranteed by governance. Here’s the minimum viable system.

Triggers (automated and human)
- Gestational age 16–24 weeks with any of: cervical dilation on exam; cervical length ≤10–15 mm with funneling and intact membranes; prior mid-trimester loss suggestive of cervical insufficiency.
- No fever, no uterine tenderness, no heavy bleeding. Rupture of membranes requires careful selection; probable infection is a hard stop.

Hard stops (single-screen checklist)
- Clinical chorioamnionitis; active labor; major hemorrhage; fetal demise; lethal anomaly; maternal instability.

One-call activation
- A single RCC pager/phone that alerts MFM/OB lead, anesthesia, OR charge, ultrasound, and NICU. No serial calls. One page, everyone moves.

Standard kit and room readiness
- Stocked RCC cart: Mersilene tape, ring forceps, speculum set, bladder catheter, tenaculum, long needle drivers, sponge sticks, balloon-tipped catheter for membrane reduction, antiseptic, antibiotics, tocolytics.
- Clear protocol for bladder filling vs amnioreduction when membranes are at the os. If amnioreduction is used at your center, script it—don’t improvise.

EHR build (this is the code we control)
- Best Practice Alert when CL ≤15 mm before 24 weeks or when “cervical dilation” + “intact membranes” documented.
- RCC order set: labs, antibiotics, tocolysis, analgesia/anesthesia, consent, device pull, pre-op timeout items, postoperative progesterone plan, follow-up ultrasound schedule.
- Smart phrases for counseling: benefits, risks (infection, rupture, laceration, suture failure), alternatives (progesterone, expectant management), evidence limits.
- Timer starts at RCC activation; stops when cerclage tied. Write the metric into the flow sheet.
- Registry fields: GA at stitch, indication, technique (McDonald/Shirodkar), membrane status, adjuncts used, complications, latency days, GA at delivery, neonatal survival. If your system can’t report it, you didn’t build it.

Clinical sequence (no debate in the hallway)
- Screen for infection and labor. If clean and eligible, activate RCC.
- Antibiotics per protocol. Consider tocolysis to quiet an irritable uterus.
- Choose technique based on anatomy and exposure. McDonald for speed and simplicity; Shirodkar when tissue support and coverage matter. Document why.
- Handle membranes with intention: Trendelenburg, bladder fill, or controlled amnioreduction if that’s a vetted option at your site.
- Tie the stitch. Confirm hemostasis. Post-op monitoring. Start or continue progesterone if aligned with your practice.
- Discharge plan with clear return precautions and follow-up.

Governance and accountability
- MFM/OB lead owns the pathway. Periop leadership guarantees “life-of-limb-of-pregnancy” access, with metrics reviewed monthly.
- Simulation every quarter for triage, setup, membrane reduction, and crisis conversion.
- Equitable access: community clinics and EDs get the same one-call number and transport protocol. No patient loses days because they live far from your tower.

Measure what matters
- Door-to-stitch time (target ≤90 minutes from activation).
- Latency days gained.
- GA at delivery.
- Maternal infection and cervical laceration rates.
- Neonatal survival to discharge.
- Case mix and equity by zip code and insurance.

What we don’t know—and what we don’t need to wait for
- The Cureus review is abstract-only. Technique superiority and exact selection thresholds remain heterogeneous across studies. Fine. We can still standardize triggers, hard stops, and execution.
- We don’t need a new RCT to fix paging trees, empty carts, or missing order sets. That is ops debt, not science.

When physicians build, the stitch stops being heroic and starts being reliable. We take variability out of the hallway and put it into a controlled checklist. We encode clinical judgment into an activation pathway that moves faster than doubt and slower than recklessness.

This is our agency. If previable birth is a tragedy, then friction is a choice. Build the pathway, wire it into the EHR, own the metrics, and audit the misses. The future of medicine is not better opinions about cerclage—it’s better systems that make the right care inevitable.
