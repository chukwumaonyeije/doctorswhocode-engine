At 2:14 p.m., a 22-week patient with 2 cm dilation and bulging membranes hits triage.
Ultrasound confirms the cervix is collapsing.
We debate room vs OR, anesthesia vs epidural, who can scrub, which suture is stocked, and whether the attending is “comfortable” with Shirodkar.
Minutes bleed into risk.

We call it clinical uncertainty.
It’s not.
It’s an architecture problem.

A 2024 Cureus review (abstract-only; PMID: 39371694; DOI: 10.7759/cureus.68619) summarizes what many of us know from years at the bedside: rescue cervical cerclage (RCC) can reduce previable birth for the right patient, at the right moment, with the right technique.
McDonald vs Shirodkar both have a place.
Risks—like infection and cervical laceration—are real but manageable when the system is tight.
The science is not perfect, and much of it is observational, but the signal is consistent enough to organize around.

We don’t fail RCC because the knot is hard.
We fail because we haven’t built the workflow.

The usual framing is “Should we place a cerclage?” as if it’s a philosophical exercise.
The right framing is “Can we execute a time-critical, high-reliability pathway from first sign of cervical failure to suture pass?”
That is software-and-operations thinking, not a purely clinical debate.

Why does the problem persist?
Because our inputs are fragmented and our incentives are misaligned.

- The EHR doesn’t treat cervical length or painless dilation as a code-red trigger. Ultrasound results sit in inboxes. Nobody is paging anesthesia when the cervix hits 10 mm.
- Indications are policy-light and personality-heavy. One attending’s “too risky” is another’s “go now.” That is governance by vibe.
- Supply and space are unreliable. The cerclage kit is in a locked cabinet. The right suture isn’t par level. The OR scheduler doesn’t see OB triage in real time.
- We don’t measure the only metric that matters: time from recognition to suture. If you don’t make time visible, you can’t compress it.
- There’s no feedback loop. Cases vanish into the ether. No registry, no postmortem, no institutional memory.

So we stall, while membranes bulge and odds worsen.
Then we blame the disease.

Here’s what changes when physicians build.

We ship a cerclage operating system, not a policy PDF.
That means a protocol expressed as event-driven automation, with explicit governance, and telemetry baked in.

- A single trigger: cervical length <15 mm without contractions, or painless dilation/membrane exposure ≤24 weeks, fires an RCC Fast Path. This is encoded in the EHR, not just a laminated card.
- Auto-page a cross-covering “cerclage stack”: OB, anesthesia, charge nurse, scrub tech, NICU, and ultrasound. The system assembles the room while humans decide the plan.
- A prebuilt order set: antibiotics, tocolysis per local practice, analgesia/anesthesia preferences, betadine, indomethacin if indicated, bladder fill or balloon for membrane reduction. No scavenger hunt.
- A stocked, sealed kit: right suture choices, retractors, tenacula, bladder catheter, sponge sticks, and a sterile condom for membrane reduction. One SKU. Daily check. No exceptions.
- Technique defaults with gates: McDonald as the default; Shirodkar when exposure or tissue demands. This is decision support, not decision replacement. Document the gate crossed.
- Time standards: goal <90 minutes from trigger to knife. Visible timer on the tracking board. Cases breaching the time budget trigger an automatic debrief.
- Consent macros that name real risks plainly—chorioamnionitis, laceration, PPROM—so we don’t sanitize the tradeoffs or slow down in paperwork.
- Post-procedure telemetry: gestational age at placement, prolongation interval, complications, delivery GA. This rolls into a local registry that updates a live dashboard.

Governance matters.
Codify who can greenlight RCC and under what conditions, and protect that decision with institutional backing.
If the policy says “OB on-call can proceed on trigger,” then the OB on-call is not left alone to negotiate with an OR board built for elective hernias.
Tie time-to-cerclage to service-level expectations the OR must meet, not best-effort favors.

Incentives matter.
If your unit reports RCC process metrics to the same execs who track door-to-balloon, you’ll get room access and inventory.
If it lives as “OB drama,” you won’t.
Make previable birth prevention a hospital KPI with a named owner.

Data matters.
We don’t need another 30-page guideline.
We need a small, open registry with fields we actually use.
Abstract-only reviews, like the Cureus paper cited here, help summarize indications, techniques, and outcomes, but they won’t reorganize a hospital.
Physician-builders will.
Start with N=every case, not N=perfect publication.
Share de-identified metrics with neighboring systems and learn faster than journals can publish.

Technique matters, but only after the system launches you into the room.
McDonald vs Shirodkar is a clinical choice based on anatomy, exposure, and membrane behavior.
The review notes both are viable with tradeoffs and lists known complications like infection and laceration.
That matches lived practice.
What widens the delta is not a suture brand; it’s a pathway that preserves the hour when the intervention still works.

Stop romanticizing “clinical judgment” while starving it of infrastructure.
Judgment operates on the runway we build.

Here’s a starter spec you can ship in 30 days:

- Build an RCC Fast Path in your EHR that fires on cervical length and dilation criteria.
- Stand up a sealed cerclage kit with daily check and documented backup location.
- Publish a one-page technique gate with images. Default McDonald; gate to Shirodkar when exposure is limited or cervix is scarred.
- Define time-to-cerclage as a unit metric. Track it publicly on your board.
- Automate a next-day 10-minute debrief for every case, hit or miss.
- Launch a simple registry: placement GA, indication, technique, antibiotics, complications, prolongation, delivery GA. Trend monthly. Share anonymized aggregates.

If you code, don’t wait for a vendor.
Use your system’s rules engine to wire the trigger.
Spin up a FHIR subscription for critical ultrasound results.
Post to a secure webhook that alerts the team.
If you don’t code, find the colleague who does and give them the map.
This is not moonshot software.
It’s wiring and will.

Limitations and honesty:
The Cureus review that prompted this piece is abstract-only.
It reports RCC efficacy in selected patients, outlines McDonald and Shirodkar techniques, and acknowledges risks like infection and cervical laceration.
Comparisons to progesterone and bed rest are described, but details are not visible without full text.
That’s fine.
The uncertainty argues for better local data capture and faster learning, not for inertia.

Physicians built neonatal ICU standards, trauma activations, and stroke alerts because we refused to accept chaos as a feature.
Rescue cerclage deserves the same discipline.
Build the path of least resistance to the suture, instrument it, and hold it to time.
That’s our job.
No one is coming to do it for us.
