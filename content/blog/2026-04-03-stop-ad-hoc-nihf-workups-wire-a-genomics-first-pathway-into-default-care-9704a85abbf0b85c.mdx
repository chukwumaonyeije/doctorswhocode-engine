The page comes at 24 weeks. Ascites, skin edema, a small pericardial effusion. The sonographer looks at me. I open the EHR and realize there is no default pathway. Just a blank order screen, a family in the room, and the clock.

We keep treating non‑immune hydrops fetalis like a riddle for a lone MFM to solve. It is not a riddle. It is a systems problem that punishes delay and ambiguity, and it puts mothers at risk.

The new SMFM Consult #75 gives us the blueprint. The failure now is not knowledge. It is execution.


## What the consult actually supports

Provenance first. This is a full‑text SMFM consult (2026) synthesizing contemporary evidence. Many recommendations are strong but rest on low‑quality data, which the document labels explicitly (GRADE C). That is our reality in rare, heterogeneous conditions.

The backbone is diagnostic. When one or more fetal effusions are detected, perform fetal diagnostic testing that includes chromosomal microarray with or without karyotype. Add PCR testing when infection is on the table. Do a detailed anatomic survey, fetal echocardiography, MCA Dopplers, and review parental labs and carrier screens.

Offer exome or genome sequencing when CMA or karyotype is non‑diagnostic. Offer it concurrently with CMA when aneuploidy risk is low or a single‑gene disorder is likely. Expect a monogenic diagnosis in at least a third of otherwise unexplained cases. RASopathies lead that list. Inborn errors of metabolism, lymphatic disorders, skeletal and neuromuscular syndromes, and hereditary anemias follow.

Maternal risk is real. Mirror syndrome hits roughly one in ten NIHF pregnancies and behaves like preeclampsia. It resolves with delivery or successful fetal intervention. The recommendation is firm: counsel about delivery or abortion care, and reserve expectant management for rare scenarios with shared decision‑making.

Management is individualized. No evidence supports elective preterm delivery for everyone with NIHF. Deliver preterm for obstetric indications like preeclampsia or mirror syndrome, spontaneous preterm labor or PPROM, new or worsening hydrops, or when the overall maternal or fetal risk of continuing exceeds delivery. Give antenatal corticosteroids if preterm delivery is likely within seven days and neonatal resuscitation is planned. Cesarean is for standard obstetric indications, not hydrops alone.

Fetal therapy is selective and meaningful. Treat SVT or atrial flutter with maternal antiarrhythmics. Do intrauterine transfusion for confirmed anemia. Drain a large effusion or place a thoracoamniotic shunt when prolonging pregnancy or enabling neonatal resuscitation is the goal. Consider steroids for large CPAMs, with procedure for macrocysts when appropriate.


## The architecture of delay

NIHF outcomes deteriorate when we let the system default to nothing. The bottlenecks are structural.

- The EHR has no pathway. Orders for CMA, infectious PCR, exome, MCA Dopplers, and fetal echo are scattered and easy to miss. No one owns a timer on “time to diagnosis.”

- Payer rules make exome an obstacle course. Prior auth burns days we do not have. Coverage is uneven, and out‑of‑pocket shocks drive inequity.

- Genetics, MFM, neonatology, and fetal therapy are siloed. Patients ping‑pong between calendars while mirror syndrome brews.

- Governance is vague. We ask clinicians to carry the ethical and legal weight of abortion access without clear institutional process, especially under changing state laws.

This is not about smarter doctors. It is about building a system that does the right thing by default.


## Build the physician‑led NIHF pathway

I want a single click from “hydrops suspected” to a complete, equitable plan. Here is what to build.

- A NIHF spectrum order set that is the default
  - CMA with reflex karyotype when indicated
  - Infectious PCR panel when clinically relevant, plus syphilis serology
  - Exome or genome sequencing workflows queued at detection, with concurrent option when single‑gene disease is likely or aneuploidy risk is low
  - Fetal echocardiogram, MCA Dopplers, detailed anatomic survey, growth, and amniotic fluid
  - Maternal labs: blood type, indirect Coombs, Kleihauer‑Betke or flow cytometry when anemia is suspected, hemoglobinopathy and alpha‑thalassemia testing logic based on MCV and ancestry

- A payer‑proof exome pre‑auth wrapper
  - Auto‑populate indications, ultrasound findings, and family history
  - Route to a centralized team that turns approvals around within 24 to 48 hours
  - Offer financial counseling and institutional coverage for the uninsured or underinsured

- A same‑day multidisciplinary console
  - MFM, fetal cardiology, genetics, neonatology, and fetal therapy on one video block
  - Standardized counseling that includes the full range of options, including abortion care and palliative pathways

- Fetal therapy triggers embedded in the record
  - SVT or atrial flutter detected, auto‑page the antiarrhythmia protocol
  - Elevated MCA PSV, prompt percutaneous sampling and transfusion pathway
  - Large effusion or CPAM metrics, surface shunt or procedure criteria

- Abortion and perinatal palliative care access as first‑class options
  - Clear, documented workflows that comply with jurisdictional rules
  - Logistics, not moral injury, should determine timing

- Perinatal pathology and specimen routing by default
  - Placenta, fetal blood, and fluid samples triaged to the right labs
  - Close the loop on diagnosis for recurrence counseling


## Measure what matters and govern it

If we do not measure, we will drift back to ad hoc care.

- Time from effusion detection to definitive diagnostic sampling
- Time from sampling to result disclosure for CMA and exome
- Exome uptake and payer approval rates by race, language, and insurance
- Rate of mirror syndrome, ICU admission, and transfusion in the birthing parent
- Appropriateness of antenatal corticosteroid use before preterm delivery
- Proportion receiving indicated fetal therapy and associated outcomes
- Delivery timing relative to obstetric indications
- Autopsy and placental pathology completion rates
- Patient‑reported experience with counseling on abortion and palliative care

Publish these internally every quarter. Tie operational fixes to misses, not to shame but to system change.


## The build is on us

NIHF is heterogeneous and unforgiving. The consult does not ask us to guess better. It asks us to diagnose faster, treat when we can, and be honest about maternal risk.

I am done with blank order screens and heroics. Physicians who build need to hardwire a genomics‑first, equity‑aware NIHF pathway into the EHR, the revenue cycle, and the call schedule. That is agency. That is our responsibility.
