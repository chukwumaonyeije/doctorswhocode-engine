I walked into clinic and my patient didn’t bring questions. She brought a plan—generated by ChatGPT, annotated, and bolded. Differential, guideline citations, a proposed workup, and a draft prior auth letter. She wasn’t asking for permission. She was asking for adjudication.

The usual framing collapses here. This isn’t a misinformation problem. It’s an architecture problem. Our systems were built to keep the patient’s computation outside the chart, outside the visit, and outside reimbursement.

Gilles Frydman highlighted a new Lancet comment making the obvious explicit: patients are not waiting for permission to use AI. The post also points to a 2025 systematic review of primary care patients’ views on AI that reportedly found zero studies of how patients actually use large language models. Zero. I have not independently verified that review; the Lancet piece is here: https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanprc/article/PIIS3050-5143(26)00007-5/fulltext. But the claim matches what we see every day: patients already use these tools to compress uncertainty into decisions before they reach us.

Let’s name why the friction persists.

- Workflow: EHRs treat outside computation like contraband. No structured field for “patient AI summary.” No way to record model, version, or prompt. Patient uploads get buried as scanned PDFs or blocked outright.

- Incentives: We reimburse “time on keyboard” when the keyboard is ours. We don’t compensate for triaging patient-supplied reasoning, even when it saves time and improves care.

- Governance: Risk policies equate “patient AI” with liability, so the default is to ignore or discourage. That posture is not safety. It is abdication.

- Culture: We embraced clinician-facing AI for notes and orders while treating patient-facing AI as something to manage away. Same paternalism, new clothes.

What makes this moment structurally different from patient portals in 2010?

- Decision compression: LLMs don’t just inform; they propose. Patients are arriving with differentials, thresholds, and plans. That changes the encounter load.

- Off-platform inevitability: On-device models and consumer apps sit outside HIPAA perimeters. You can’t gate them with portal design or policy memos.

- Parallel rails: Direct-to-consumer labs, imaging, and prescribing are scaling. Patients can generate data, interpretations, and actions before we see them.

Old tactics—delay, deflect, label as “not in workflow”—will be bypassed. The triad is already in the room. We either architect for it or we get worked around.

Here is what changes when physicians build.

1) Add an AI intake lane
- Accept patient AI artifacts as first-class clinical inputs using FHIR DocumentReference.
- Required metadata: model name, version/date, prompt, sources cited.
- Surface it to the clinician pre-visit like outside records, not as a buried message thread.

2) Compensate adjudication
- Map review time to existing codes (e.g., 99421–99423 for e-visits, 99417 prolonged services, G2211 complexity add-on) and write local policy that “patient-supplied AI reasoning” qualifies when documented.
- If your billing team balks, fix the policy, not the patient.

3) Structure the note for triadic care
- Add three fixed sections:
  - Patient AI summary (one paragraph, with provenance).
  - Clinician adjudication (correct/partial/unsafe with rationale).
  - Agreed plan and uncertainties.
- This is not extra documentation. It replaces hedging in the assessment with transparent reasoning.

4) Build safety where it belongs
- No unsourced model outputs get copied verbatim into orders.
- If patient AI proposes off-label or high-risk steps, the EHR forces an adjudication checkbox plus brief rationale before signing.

5) Teach critical AI health literacy
- Publish one-page micro-guides in your portal: how to ask, how to cite, how to carry uncertainty into the visit.
- Provide a standard pre-visit prompt template that elicits timelines, meds, red flags, and goals. Help patients ask better questions so we can give better answers.

6) Instrument the new workflow
- Create a registry field: “Patient used AI (Y/N),” model, and whether adjudication changed management.
- Close the research gap with pragmatic data instead of symposia. Start with N=100 at your clinic. Submit the results.

7) Update governance with patients at the table
- Stand up a triadic care committee with patient researchers.
- Align malpractice language to recognize adjudication as a protective act, not a liability magnet.
- Publish a one-page policy: we welcome patient AI; here’s how we handle it safely.

8) Don’t wait for your vendor
- You can prototype most of this with existing tools: a smartphrase, a portal form, and a routing rule.
- If IT says “not on the roadmap,” your roadmap is wrong. Build the lightweight version this month and backfill integrations later.

Etiquette for the AI-enabled visit fits on an index card:
- Start with, “Show me what you brought.” 
- Ask, “What outcome did you optimize for?” 
- Close with, “Here’s what I accept, what I reject, and what we’ll watch.”

None of this requires a moonshot. It requires agency. Physicians own clinical workflow. If we keep pretending patient AI is an edge case, we will keep practicing in a system that is hostile to patient reasoning and blind to its value.

Patients already seat the third participant. Our responsibility is to build the chair, write the rules, and make the room safer because of it—not in spite of it.
