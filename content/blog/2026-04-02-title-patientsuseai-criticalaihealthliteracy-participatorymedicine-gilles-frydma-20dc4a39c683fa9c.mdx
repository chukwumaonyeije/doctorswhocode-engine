I walked into clinic and met a patient holding a printed plan. It wasn’t from WebMD. It was a clean HPI, a three-item differential, red flags to monitor, and questions for me—generated by an LLM. The visit started with a decision on the table, not a blank screen.

We keep framing this as a risk to manage. That’s the wrong lens. This is a workflow and governance gap. Patients use AI. The only question is whether our systems know what to do with it.

A recent Lancet comment argued that the knowledge monopoly is gone and triadic care—clinician, patient, AI—is the new baseline. That aligns with what I’m seeing in rooms and inboxes. Note: the Lancet piece is a comment, not a randomized trial. We should treat it as a directional thesis, not dispositive evidence.

The paternalism is familiar. Clinicians quietly adopt AI for documentation and coding, then bristle when patients show up with AI outputs. Same tech. Two standards. If we defend that with “safety,” we’re hiding governance with rhetoric.

Why this persists:
- Architecture: EHRs are not designed to ingest, label, and compare patient AI artifacts. So they get treated as email clutter.
- Incentives: Throughput and RVUs reward control and speed, not co-production. Reviewing patient-generated plans looks like “extra work.”
- Policy: Many orgs treat patient-generated data as second-class—accepted from devices with vendor agreements, ignored when it comes from the patient’s brain or browser.
- Liability folklore: “If it’s in the chart, you own it,” so we keep it out. That’s not risk management. That’s documentation theater.

What changes when physicians build:
We stop debating if AI is “in the room” and make the room ready for it. That means a clinical architecture, not a one-off pep talk.

The intake
- Ask it explicitly: “Did you use AI to prepare for this visit?” Don’t make patients confess; make it standard.
- Capture the artifact as data, not a PDF blob.
  - Structure: HPI, meds, allergies, red flags considered, sources cited, and the patient’s goals of care.
  - Metadata: model name, version, date/time, prompt summary. Store as a DocumentReference linked to the Encounter. If you speak FHIR, you know the drill.
- Offer a “prompt prescription” in the portal so patients can generate safer, structured briefs:
  - “Summarize my symptoms in 5 sentences.”
  - “List three possible causes and what would make each more or less likely.”
  - “List danger signs that should send me to the ER.”
  - “Draft questions for my clinician.”

The visit
- Side-by-side view: patient AI plan vs. guideline pathway. Not a vibe check. A diff.
- Clinician actions: Accept, Modify, or Reject each item with a one-line rationale. That creates an audit trail and teaches the patient how we think.
- Time box it. Two minutes to scan, one minute to decide. This is not an extra 15 minutes. It’s focused adjudication.
- Auto-generate the After Visit Summary by merging agreed items, with “We changed X because Y.” That’s safety and learning, not paternalism.

The record
- Preserve the artifact with provenance. If your policy can’t tolerate that, fix the policy. Model versioning and timestamps matter.
- Do not let AI overwrite clinician authorship. The doctor is still the author of record; the AI artifact is a cited source, like a consult note.

Safety and ethics
- Guardrails are work, not slogans:
  - High-risk domains (oncology staging, anticoagulation, pediatrics) trigger mandatory clinician review and documented overrides.
  - Source alerts when the patient artifact cites no references or cites junk.
  - Bias and equity checks in QI: track whether AI-informed visits differ by language, literacy, or access.
- Consent to compute: tell patients what models are in play, where their text goes, and how we de-identify. If you can’t state it plainly, don’t deploy it.

Measurement
- Instrument the flow. Metrics that matter:
  - Diagnostic changes attributable to patient artifacts.
  - Visit cycle time with vs. without artifact review.
  - Post-visit clarifying messages per 100 visits.
  - Patient-reported understanding and confidence.
  - Safety events and near-misses tied to AI content, clinician- or patient-initiated.

Payment and policy
- Today: document the review as part of medical decision making. It’s not “extra.” It is the visit.
- Tomorrow: push for an “AI-informed visit” modifier that recognizes adjudication work. If payers can create modifiers for telehealth and prolonged services, they can do it here.

Education
- Teach critical AI health literacy to both sides. Patients need to know how to ask, not just how to search. Clinicians need to know how to inspect, not just how to dismiss.
- Publish prompt templates, not platitudes. Share failure cases so others don’t repeat them.

Build it with the stack you already have
- Patient portal: FHIR Questionnaire + CommunicationRequest to submit AI artifacts.
- Storage: DocumentReference with extensions for model metadata.
- Linking: tie to Encounter and Condition for longitudinal context.
- UI: a simple diff view and Accept/Modify/Reject buttons. Ship ugly v1. Iterate under QI, not stealth.

Provenance and limits
- This argument was sparked by a public post from Gilles Frydman referencing a Lancet comment on AI-empowered patients and noting a gap in studies of how patients use large language models. I have not independently audited the cited 2025 systematic review. The Lancet piece is a comment, not primary research. The operational patterns here come from clinic reality and build work, not a meta-analysis.

The deeper shift
One commenter put it well: AI compresses judgment. Patients aren’t just arriving with more information; they’re arriving with a formed plan. Portals never did that. Direct-to-consumer labs plus AI interpretation will.

Our response cannot be to quarantine the plan at the doorway. That’s control theater. The work is to make the plan inspectable, comparable, and either adoptable or refutable—fast, auditable, and safe.

If we don’t design this, others will. They will design it around billing, clicks, or ad tech. We will inherit it and complain about burden.

Physicians built medicine’s best systems when we acted as stewards of both science and workflow. That mandate hasn’t changed. Patients will bring the AI. Our job is to build the visit that earns their trust and improves their outcomes. Agency is ours. Responsibility is ours. The future of medicine belongs to the people who ship this well.
