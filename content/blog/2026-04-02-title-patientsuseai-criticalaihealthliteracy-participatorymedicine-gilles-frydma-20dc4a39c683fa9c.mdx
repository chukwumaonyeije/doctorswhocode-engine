A patient walks in with a ChatGPT summary, a differential, and a stepwise plan they intend to follow. Not “I Googled this.” A compressed judgment with citations, thresholds, and a preferred imaging study booked at a cash lab. We can pretend this is fringe. It isn’t.

The usual framing says “misinformation risk.” That’s too small. This is an architecture problem. We built clinics, EHRs, billing, and governance for a dyad. Care is now triadic: patient, clinician, AI. Our systems still only seat two.

Gilles Frydman flagged a Lancet comment that states the obvious with overdue clarity: patients are not waiting for permission to use AI. He also points to a 2025 review in primary care that, reportedly, found zero studies of how patients actually use large language models. Zero. I have not independently verified that review; if true, it’s damning and explains why our policies feel unmoored from reality.

Let’s name the structural blockers:
- Payment rewards volume, not synthesis. There’s no routine place to bill time spent auditing patient-supplied reasoning.
- EHRs treat outside text like biohazard. Free text gets quarantined, red flags don’t auto-surface, provenance is lost.
- Risk management punishes engagement with third-party outputs. The safest note is “patient advised against online advice,” even when the advice is sensible.
- Governance is paternalistic by design. Committees that greenlight clinician AI ban patient AI in the same breath.
- Research avoids the messy middle. IRBs see “patients using AI” as a subject risk, not a chance to co-produce knowledge.

What’s new isn’t “empowered patients.” We’ve had that since WebMD. What’s new is decision compression. Generative tools turn scattered facts into an actionable plan before the visit. Add direct-to-consumer testing and you get patients arriving with data, interpretation, and intent. That shifts the locus of control whether we like it or not.

Safety matters. But “guardrails first” has become an excuse for stasis. We can build for safety and agency at once. That starts with admitting the knowledge monopoly is gone and designing the visit around that truth.

Here’s the minimum viable architecture for triadic care.

- Create a visit type: AI-informed consult
  - Schedule with a pre-visit review block.
  - Make plan reconciliation the primary goal, not reenacting HPI theater.

- Ingest patient AI outputs as structured data
  - Accept a short “reasoning bundle” before the visit with fields we can parse.
  - Keep provenance: model name, version, timestamp, prompt.

Example intake skeleton (keep it boring, keep it parseable):
- patient_goals: top 3
- symptoms: timeline, severity, red flags checked yes/no
- working_hypotheses: list with confidence 0–1
- key_evidence: labs/imaging with source links
- uncertainties: what would change the plan
- model_meta: model name, version, date, prompt hash, links

- Build a two-pane view in the chart
  - Left: patient/AI claims and plan. Right: clinician assessment and reconciled plan.
  - Auto-highlight conflicts and missing data. Generate explicit “accepted,” “modified,” “rejected” markers with rationale.

- Codify consent and documentation
  - One-click consent: “We discussed third-party AI outputs; I reviewed, verified critical elements, and documented reconciliation.”
  - Log the model and prompt. Paste the reasoning bundle to a non-billable, non-indexed attachment with a human-readable summary in the note.

- Ship safety prompts, not scolds
  - Inline checks tied to red flags: “If X + Y, escalate now.” Not modal nags.
  - Default to ordering verification, not duplication. If patient brings clean, recent labs, justify repeats.

- Align with existing billing without gamesmanship
  - Use recognized data-review and prolonged-service pathways where appropriate.
  - If your payer mix blocks this, escalate as an operational risk: uncompensated safety work.

- Measure what matters
  - Plan convergence time (minutes to aligned plan).
  - Avoidable duplicate testing rate.
  - Patient-reported understanding and confidence post-visit.
  - Near-miss and harm attribution that explicitly tags third-party AI and clinician reconciliation steps.

- Train both sides
  - Clinician micro-skills: fast provenance check, bias spotting, uncertainty surfacing, respectful plan negotiation.
  - Patient one-pager: how to bring an AI plan that’s auditable, not a wall of text.

- Governance that fits the work
  - Approve “triadic visit” as a standard modality.
  - Require model provenance logging, not model pre-approval for patients.
  - Stand up a near-miss registry for AI-involved cases with a blameless review loop.

If you’re a physician who codes, this is buildable in 90 days.

- 0–30 days
  - Define the intake spec and the two-pane UI.
  - Add a lightweight consent macro and provenance fields to your note template.
  - Pilot with one clinic, five clinicians, and willing patients.

- 31–60 days
  - Wire alerts for red-flag mismatches.
  - Stand up a minimal registry for outcomes and near-misses.
  - Present to risk leadership with real cases and early metrics.

- 61–90 days
  - Publish the intake spec and templates openly.
  - Expand to additional clinics. File payer test claims with clear documentation of non-face-to-face review time where applicable.
  - Share the safety data, not marketing slides.

Provenance and limits matter. The Lancet piece referenced by Frydman argues clinicians should welcome AI-informed patients and accept triadic care as the new baseline. I agree with the direction. The claim about a 2025 primary care review finding no studies of patient LLM use comes via his post; I haven’t audited the review. Regardless, the clinic is already telling us the story: patients are compressing judgment before they see us. Our systems are the bottleneck.

When physicians build, the conversation changes. We stop gatekeeping and start governing. We replace paternalism with auditable process. We reduce risk by making patient reasoning visible, not by pretending it doesn’t exist.

The conclusion is simple. Agency has shifted. Our responsibility is to design care that recognizes it. If we don’t, payers and platforms will design it for us—and not in the patient’s or the clinician’s interest.
