## Why this matters

Hashimoto’s thyroiditis (HT) is common, heterogeneous, and easy to miss—especially when antibodies are negative or imaging is equivocal. A 2024 Cureus narrative review (abstract only) synthesizes diagnostic challenges and argues for standardized criteria and future molecular/AI directions. For clinician builders, this is a chance to tighten workflows, data capture, and decision support without overreaching the evidence.

## What the review covers (per abstract)

- Framing: HT has diverse clinical presentations and overlaps with other autoimmune thyroid diseases, producing diagnostic uncertainty.
- Current approach: Diagnosis is multifactorial—clinical features, labs, and imaging.
- Antibodies: Anti–thyroid peroxidase (TPO) and antithyroglobulin antibodies are central to confirming autoimmune etiology.
- Seronegative HT: Some patients lack detectable antibodies, complicating case identification.
- Consequences: Diagnostic ambiguity can delay care or lead to misdiagnosis.
- Future directions: Standardized diagnostic criteria; deeper molecular/cellular and genetic insights; interest in thyroid regeneration; “artificial intelligence” referenced by the title but not elaborated in the abstract.

## Core diagnostic components emphasized

- Clinical features: Hypothyroid symptoms, goiter, and heterogeneity of presentation.
- Laboratory testing: TSH/free T4 for thyroid function; TPO and thyroglobulin antibodies to support autoimmune etiology.
- Imaging: Thyroid ultrasound to characterize parenchymal changes when the diagnosis is uncertain.
- Diagnostic gaps: Seronegative HT and overlapping phenotypes with other thyroid disorders.

## Gaps and limitations highlighted by the authors

- Seronegative disease remains a blind spot for antibody-based confirmation.
- Heterogeneous manifestations undermine one-size-fits-all criteria and treatment targeting.
- The review calls for standardized diagnostic criteria to reduce subjectivity.
- Future research directions include molecular/genetic characterization and regenerative approaches.

## Practical implications for clinicians

- Maintain a broad differential in patients with compatible symptoms and goiter, even when TPO/Tg antibodies are negative.
- Use ultrasound judiciously when the clinical picture and labs diverge; integrate findings with function tests rather than treating imaging in isolation.
- Revisit diagnoses over time—serologies, thyroid function, and ultrasound features can evolve.
- Document phenotype consistently (symptoms, goiter, family/autoimmune history, antibody titers, ultrasound descriptors) to aid longitudinal assessment.

## Physician‑builder opportunities

- Order sets and reflex logic
  - Create a reflex workflow linking TSH abnormalities to free T4, then to TPO/Tg antibodies when autoimmune disease is suspected.
  - Include ultrasound as a conditional add‑on when clinical and serologic data conflict.

- Structured data capture
  - Use discrete fields for antibody titers (not just positive/negative), ultrasound echotexture, vascularity, pseudonodularity, and gland volume.
  - Add a “seronegative suspected HT” flag when clinical features and ultrasound are suggestive but antibodies are absent.

- Decision support (conservative, evidence‑aligned)
  - Noninterruptive reminders to reassess diagnosis when discordant data persist (e.g., elevated TSH with negative antibodies and hypoechoic thyroid on ultrasound).
  - Longitudinal views that plot TSH, free T4, and antibody titers alongside ultrasound reports.

- Phenotype registries and quality improvement
  - Build a lightweight HT registry that distinguishes seropositive vs seronegative cases and captures ultrasound patterns.
  - Track time‑to‑diagnosis and misclassification rates to target workflow refinements.

- AI readiness (acknowledging abstract limitations)
  - Standardize data elements (antibody titers, ultrasound descriptors, symptoms) to enable future model development.
  - Consider NLP pipelines to extract ultrasound features, but position as exploratory until validated.
  - Use model governance checklists early (data provenance, bias assessment, external validation plans), given the review’s call—without specifics—for AI exploration.

## Sensible next steps

- Define local, transparent diagnostic heuristics for suspected HT that gracefully handle seronegative scenarios.
- Tighten structured documentation for ultrasound and antibody results.
- Launch a QI project to quantify discordant cases and iterate CDS triggers with end‑user feedback.
- If pursuing AI pilots, start with risk stratification or phenotyping prototypes and pre‑specify validation against clinician‑adjudicated gold standards.

## Conclusion

This Cureus narrative review (abstract only) reinforces what frontline clinicians see: HT diagnosis is multifactorial, antibodies are helpful but imperfect, seronegative disease is real, and heterogeneity impedes standardized care. While the title signals artificial intelligence, the abstract offers no model‑level details; its strongest actionable message is the need for clearer, standardized criteria and better phenotyping. Physician‑builders can add value now by improving data capture, reflex testing logic, and conservative CDS—laying the groundwork for rigorous studies and any future AI that the full body of evidence may eventually support.
